## Introduction

The introduction argues that pharma commercial AI needs an external innovation-lab discipline: Fellows can scan emerging research, test whether it improves marketing lift or brand formation, and hand only the credible ideas to product teams.

## Chapters

1. **The Pharma Marketing Machine** — Builds the reader's working map of detailing, DTC, HCP targeting, patient journeys, payer constraints, and the commercial incentives that make pharma marketing unusually data-rich and ethically fraught.

2. **From Campaign Lift to Brand Equity** — Separates short-term script lift from long-term brand measurement: NBRx, TRx, prescriber loyalty, share of voice, share of mind, brand imagery, and patient persistence.

3. **The HCP Identity Graph** — Explains how NPI-level targeting, claims data, CRM activity, endemic media behavior, EHR triggers, and physician master data become the targeting substrate for programmatic HCP marketing.

4. **What the Current AI Stack Actually Does** — Surveys next-best action, predictive audience modeling, trigger-based point-of-care messaging, MLR acceleration, field-force intelligence, and omnichannel orchestration.

5. **Ensembles, Boosting, and the Tabular Data Advantage** — Teaches why tree-based ensembles remain the practical baseline for propensity scoring, NPI targeting, and commercial prediction on structured pharma data.

6. **Mixture of Experts and Related Routing Models** — Explains MoE architecture, sparse routing, expert collapse, load balancing, and when MoE is technically meaningful versus when the phrase is just vendor language for stacked models.

7. **Uplift Modeling and Incrementality** — Shows how Fellows can move from "who is likely to prescribe?" to "who changes behavior because of this intervention?" using causal inference, uplift modeling, holdouts, and experimental design.

8. **Brand Association as a Measurable Outcome** — Designs ways to test whether AI-driven content changes perceived quality, patient-type association, message recall, confidence, and "Brand X is for this patient" mental availability.

9. **LLMs for Commercial Research and Content Intelligence** — Covers LLM-assisted literature scanning, claims-library mapping, creative evaluation, message variation, bias testing, and why human expert review remains necessary.

10. **The External Innovation Lab Operating Model** — Defines the Fellow workflow: scout research, state commercial hypothesis, design a low-cost test, score evidence quality, identify compliance risk, and write a product handoff memo.

11. **Fellow Research Portfolio** — Provides concrete research projects: MoE-vs-ensemble benchmarks, uplift experiments, brand-association studies, HCP journey simulations, EHR-message ethics audits, LLM brand-quality measurement, and patient-centered KPI pilots.

12. **From Prototype to Product Decision** — Teaches how to decide whether a promising Fellow project deserves company resources, productization, MLR review, engineering investment, client testing, or rejection.

## Notes on Order

<!-- Why are the chapters in THIS order? What does each chapter
     assume the reader has already read? If you can swap two chapters
     without breaking anything, ask whether the order is doing real work. -->

The sequence is problem-to-solution with a spiral return to measurement. Chapters 1-4 establish the commercial system before models appear, because model choice is meaningless without knowing what the system is optimizing. Chapters 5-9 teach the model and measurement toolkit. Chapters 10-12 turn that toolkit into an external innovation-lab process that can generate research briefs, prototypes, and product handoffs.

The core assessment arc is: map the system, choose the metric, choose the model, design the test, interpret the evidence, and decide whether to hand it to product.
