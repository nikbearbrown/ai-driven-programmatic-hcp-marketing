# Deep Research Prompt: Pharma Marketing — What Works, What Doesn't, Who Benefits

## Research Question

What does the empirical evidence say about pharmaceutical marketing effectiveness — to physicians, patients, and payers — including which tactics work, which don't, why, and who actually benefits from each?

---

## Core Threads to Investigate

### 1. Direct-to-Physician (DTP) Marketing

- What is the measured effect of pharmaceutical sales rep visits (detailing) on prescribing behavior? What does the pre- and post-Sunshine Act literature show?
- How does branded drug prescribing compare to generic prescribing among physicians who receive vs. do not receive pharma marketing exposure? Cite longitudinal and natural experiment studies.
- What does the evidence say about small gifts, meals, and speaker fees on prescribing? How large is the effect size and how persistent is it?
- How do specialty physicians (cardiologists, oncologists, neurologists) differ from GPs in susceptibility to pharma marketing? Is the "specialists know better" assumption empirically supported?
- What is the role of Key Opinion Leaders (KOLs) in pharma marketing — how are they identified, compensated, and what is their measured influence on peer prescribing?
- How do formulary constraints interact with pharma marketing? When do institutional protocols override individual physician influence?

### 2. Digital and Programmatic HCP Marketing

- What is the evidence base for digital programmatic advertising to physicians (point-of-care, EHR-integrated, endemic platforms)? How does it compare to traditional detailing in effect size?
- What does the literature say about physician attention to and recall of digital pharma advertising vs. traditional rep visits?
- How do EHR-integrated ads (the core Doceree model) affect prescribing at the point of care? Are there studies on real-time clinical decision influence?
- What are the compliance and ethical concerns specific to digital HCP targeting using prescribing data?

### 3. Direct-to-Consumer (DTC) Advertising

- The US and New Zealand are the only countries that allow DTC pharma advertising. What does the comparative international evidence say about whether DTC improves patient outcomes or primarily drives demand for branded over generic drugs?
- What is the measured effect of DTC advertising on physician prescribing? How often do patients who request a specific drug by name actually receive it?
- What does the evidence say about information quality in DTC ads — do they accurately convey efficacy and risk, and how do patients process the mandated side-effect disclosures?
- How does DTC advertising affect health-seeking behavior, both positively (reducing underdiagnosis) and negatively (over-medicalization)?

### 4. Information Quality and Bias

- How does pharma-sponsored medical education (CME, journal supplements, conference sponsorship) compare to independent medical education in accuracy and prescribing influence?
- What does the literature say about publication bias in pharma-sponsored clinical trials? How does selective reporting affect physician information quality?
- What is the evidence on physician self-assessment of pharma marketing influence — do physicians accurately report their own susceptibility?
- How do medical schools and residency programs currently approach pharma marketing literacy? What interventions have been shown to reduce undue influence?

### 5. Who Benefits — Stakeholder Analysis

- Under what conditions does pharma marketing produce genuine patient benefit (awareness of underdiagnosed conditions, access to genuinely superior drugs)?
- Under what conditions does it produce harm (over-prescription of expensive branded equivalents, off-label promotion, delayed generic adoption)?
- What does the health economics literature say about the aggregate cost to the healthcare system of branded drug preference driven by marketing rather than clinical superiority?
- Who captures the value: pharma companies, physicians (via speaker fees/KOL payments), platform intermediaries (Doceree, etc.), insurers, patients?
- What is the distributional effect — which patient populations bear the highest cost burden from branded-over-generic prescribing influenced by marketing?

### 6. Regulatory and Ethical Landscape

- What has been the measurable effect of the Physician Payments Sunshine Act on prescribing behavior and marketing tactics?
- How do FDA regulations on pharma advertising (fair balance, substantiation requirements) compare to actual ad content in practice? What enforcement actions exist?
- What ethical frameworks do medical associations (AMA, ACC, ACS) apply to physician-pharma relationships, and how well are they followed?
- How does the US regulatory environment compare to the EU, Canada, and Australia on pharma marketing restrictions?

### 7. Emerging and Contested Terrain

- What does early evidence say about AI-driven pharma marketing (predictive targeting, personalization at the HCP level)? What are the amplification risks?
- Is there a legitimate model for AI-powered medical information delivery to physicians that is genuinely educational rather than promotional? What would it look like and who would fund it?
- What does the health literacy literature say about the gap between what pharma marketing communicates and what patients and physicians actually need to make good decisions?

---

## Key Sources to Prioritize

- Cochrane reviews on pharmaceutical promotion and prescribing
- JAMA, NEJM, BMJ studies on detailing and prescribing behavior
- Health Affairs on DTC advertising and health economics
- ProPublica Dollars for Docs / CMS Open Payments database analyses
- Naci & Ioannidis comparative effectiveness literature
- Marcia Angell, *The Truth About the Drug Companies* (critical perspective)
- PhRMA and industry self-regulatory frameworks (primary source, note bias)
- Aaron Kesselheim (Harvard) on pharmaceutical policy
- IQVIA (formerly IMS Health) industry data on marketing spend and ROI

---

## Output Format Requested

Produce a structured evidence synthesis with:

1. A findings summary table: tactic × effect size × evidence quality × who benefits
2. Narrative sections for each of the seven threads above
3. A "what the industry claims vs. what evidence shows" comparison for each major tactic
4. An honest assessment of where evidence is genuinely thin or contested vs. where there is reasonable consensus
5. Implications for AI-powered HCP marketing platforms specifically
6. A section on what a genuinely patient-centered pharma information system would look like vs. the current model

Flag where evidence is primarily from industry-funded sources and where independent replication exists or is absent.
