# Research Notes: Chapter 12 — Research Design and Public Data for Marketing Science

**Chapter one-line (TIKTOC):** Fellows learn the causal-inference toolkit (natural experiments, difference-in-differences, regression discontinuity, instrumental variables) and map public data sources to research questions.

**Learning outcomes (TIKTOC):** (1, Apply) Select an identification strategy for a question given available public data. (2, Analyze) Evaluate the identifying assumptions each design requires against the pharma context. (3, Create / Part B) Design a credibly identified study for the Fellow's own thread on public data.

**Opening artifact (TIKTOC):** a state detailing-restriction policy change as a natural experiment (before/after, neighbor-state control).

**Core content (TIKTOC):** the identification problem; natural experiments (disclosure laws, AMC access rules, formulary changes, LOE); DiD with staggered adoption; RDD at formulary/payment thresholds; IV; the public data stack — CMS Open Payments, Medicare Part D prescriber data, ICER value assessments, state Medicaid claims.

**Scope of these notes:** causal identification for marketing science (the four designs + their assumptions and pharma-specific threats), the canonical published pharma-marketing natural experiments, and the public-data stack (what each dataset contains, coverage years, access, and which questions each supports). This is the methods-and-data chapter that arms the Ch 13 portfolio.

## A. Conceptual foundations

### A1. The identification problem (why prediction ≠ causation, restated for design)
**Explanation.** Ch 8 established the inversion (propensity ≠ incrementality). Ch 12 supplies the *designs* that recover causal effects when a randomized holdout is unavailable — which, on public data, it almost always is. The fundamental problem of causal inference: for any unit you observe either the treated or the untreated potential outcome, never both. Every identification strategy is a different argument for how to *impute the missing counterfactual* from observable units. Natural experiments find a treatment assignment that is "as good as random" with respect to the outcome; DiD imputes the counterfactual trend from a control group; RDD imputes it from units just on the other side of a threshold; IV imputes it from variation driven by an instrument that affects the outcome only through the treatment.
**Common misconception.** "With enough controls in a regression, I have a causal estimate." No — controlling for observed confounders does nothing about *unobserved* ones (physician quality, patient mix, local market dynamics). The designs below are valuable precisely because they neutralize unobserved confounding through structure (a policy shock, a threshold, an instrument), not through a longer covariate list.
**Source.** `pantry/_lib_ai-applied-causal-inference-powered-by-ml-and-ai.md` (Ch 16 DiD; the parallel-trends / ATET identification argument); `pantry/_lib_math-causal-inference-mit-press-essential-knowledge-series.md` (natural-experiments framing). The DML-for-DiD treatment in the applied-causal-inference book is the bridge to letting the Fellow control for high-dimensional covariates flexibly (Section 16.3).

### A2. Natural experiments (the workhorse for public-data pharma research)
**Explanation.** A natural experiment exploits a real-world event that assigns "treatment" to some units and not others in a way plausibly unrelated to the outcome. In pharma HCP marketing the recurring sources are:
- **State detailing/disclosure & gift-ban laws** — Minnesota (gift-disclosure since 1993; gift cap), Vermont (2009 gift ban + disclosure), and others create cross-state variation in how much commercial promotion physicians can receive. Compare prescribing in regulated vs. unregulated states.
- **Academic-medical-center (AMC) detailing-access rules** — between 2006 and 2012, many AMCs enacted policies restricting pharma-rep visits. Physicians at restricting AMCs are "treated"; physicians at non-restricting AMCs (or the same physicians before/after) are controls. This is the canonical Larkin et al. (2017) design (B1).
- **Formulary changes** — a payer moving a drug across tiers, or imposing/removing prior authorization, shifts the cost/friction a physician faces. Coverage changes at known dates create before/after contrasts.
- **Loss of exclusivity (LOE) / generic entry** — a dated, exogenous economic shock to a brand; generic entry changes substitution economics overnight and is a clean event for studying brand-equity persistence (see Ch 13 Track B, and B4 below).
**Common misconception.** "Any before/after comparison is a natural experiment." No — the assignment must be plausibly exogenous to the outcome trend. A policy adopted *because* prescribing was already changing is endogenous and invalidates the design. Always ask: why did *these* units get treated *when* they did, and is that reason correlated with the outcome?
**Worked example (the opener).** A state enacts a gift ban at a known date; neighboring states do not. Compare the change in branded-drug share among the state's prescribers (Part D + State Drug Utilization Data) to the change among neighbor-state prescribers over the same window. The neighbor states supply the counterfactual trend.
**Source.** Larkin et al. 2017 (B1); Minnesota/Vermont disclosure-law literature (Ross et al., *JAMA* 2007, "Pharmaceutical company payments to physicians: early experiences with disclosure laws in Vermont and Minnesota," https://jamanetwork.com/journals/jama/fullarticle/206127). `pantry/pharma-marketing-evidence-synthesis.md` §1 (detailing works; Sunshine Act = disclosure without behavior change).

### A3. Difference-in-differences (DiD), including staggered adoption
**Explanation.** DiD compares the *change* in outcome for a treated group to the *change* for a control group across a pre/post boundary. The double-differencing removes any time-invariant differences between groups and any common time shock. The identifying assumption is **parallel trends**: absent treatment, the treated group's outcome would have evolved along the same path as the control group's. Under parallel trends, the difference-in-differences identifies the **Average Treatment effect on the Treated (ATET)**.
- **Two-group/two-period canonical case:** clean, but rare in policy data.
- **Staggered adoption:** units adopt the policy at different dates (e.g., AMCs restricting detailing across 2006–2012; states passing gift laws in different years). This is the realistic pharma case. The naive fix — two-way fixed effects (TWFE) regression — is now known to be **biased under heterogeneous treatment effects**, because already-treated units get used as controls for later-treated units ("forbidden comparisons," the Goodman-Bacon decomposition). Modern estimators (Callaway & Sant'Anna 2021; de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille 2020/2022; Sun & Abraham 2021; Borusyak et al.) fix this by building clean group-time ATTs and using only never-treated or not-yet-treated units as controls.
**Common misconception.** "Run a TWFE regression with unit and time fixed effects and you've done staggered DiD." This was standard practice and is now understood to be wrong when effects vary across cohorts or over time. Fellows must use a heterogeneity-robust estimator and report an event-study plot to show pre-trends.
**Diagnostics required:** (a) an **event-study / pre-trends plot** (leads should be ~0); (b) parallel-trends is *functional-form dependent* — parallel trends in levels need not hold in logs, so the outcome transformation is a real modeling choice; (c) report the estimator and the comparison group (never-treated vs. not-yet-treated).
**Source.** Callaway & Sant'Anna, "Difference-in-Differences with multiple time periods," *Journal of Econometrics* 225(2), 2021, pp. 200–230 (https://doi.org/10.1016/j.jeconom.2020.12.001; `did` R package, https://bcallaway11.github.io/did/). de Chaisemartin & D'Haultfœuille, *Econometrics Journal* 2022 (survey, cited in `_lib_ai-applied-causal-inference-powered-by-ml-and-ai.md` ref [17]). Goodman-Bacon decomposition and Wooldridge TWFE/Mundlak both referenced in the same `_lib_` file (refs [4], staggered-adoption discussion at its Ch 16). King et al. 2013 (B2) is a published DiD on gift-restriction policy.

### A4. Regression discontinuity design (RDD)
**Explanation.** RDD exploits a rule that assigns treatment based on whether a continuous "running variable" crosses a **threshold**. Units just below and just above the cutoff are nearly identical except for treatment status, so the jump in outcome at the cutoff identifies a local causal effect (LATE at the threshold). Pharma/health thresholds that make RDD viable:
- **Medicare Part D coverage-gap ("donut hole") spending threshold** — out-of-pocket cost jumps when cumulative spending crosses the gap entry point; drug utilization, discontinuation, and switching change sharply (Polinski et al. 2011; coverage-gap RDD literature).
- **Low-Income Subsidy (LIS) eligibility income threshold** — beneficiaries just above vs. below the income cutoff face different out-of-pocket costs; used to estimate price elasticity of adherence (e.g., the Gleevec/CML and oral-antidiabetic RDD studies).
- **Formulary tier / prior-authorization thresholds** — coverage rules that flip at a defined criterion.
**Common misconception.** "RDD needs a randomized component." No — it needs a *sharp, externally enforced cutoff* and a smooth running variable; identification comes from continuity of potential outcomes at the cutoff. The key threat is **manipulation/sorting** around the threshold (test with a density/McCrary check) and confounders that also jump at the same cutoff.
**Source.** Polinski et al., "Changes in Drug Utilization during a Gap in Insurance Coverage: ... Medicare Part D Coverage Gap" (PMC3156689, https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC3156689/); LIS-threshold RDD on diabetic adherence (PMC7741637); Gleevec/CML price-elasticity RDD (arXiv 2305.06076 — `[verify]` peer-review status before citing as established). RDD treatment in `_lib_ai-applied-causal-inference-powered-by-ml-and-ai.md` (13 mentions) and `_lib_math-causal-inference-mit-press-essential-knowledge-series.md`.

### A5. Instrumental variables (IV)
**Explanation.** When treatment is endogenous (chosen, not assigned) and no clean natural experiment exists, an **instrument** Z can recover a causal effect if it: (1) is *relevant* (predicts the treatment), (2) satisfies the *exclusion restriction* (affects the outcome only through the treatment), and (3) is *as-good-as-randomly assigned* (independent of unobserved confounders). IV estimates a LATE — the effect for "compliers" whose treatment status responds to the instrument. In pharma-marketing public-data work, candidate instruments are scarce and the exclusion restriction is usually the hard sell; plausible families include geographic/temporal variation in promotion exposure driven by something exogenous (e.g., rollout order, distance to a detailing hub) — each must be argued case by case.
**Common misconception.** "Any variable correlated with treatment is an instrument." No — relevance is testable (first-stage F; weak instruments bias the estimate toward OLS) but the exclusion restriction is fundamentally *untestable* and must be defended on substantive grounds. IV is the design most easily abused; flag it for heavy scrutiny in any Fellow brief.
**Source.** IV treatment is the most-cited method in `_lib_ai-applied-causal-inference-powered-by-ml-and-ai.md` (33 mentions) and appears in `_lib_math-causal-inference.md`, `_lib_science-the-book-of-why-the-new-science-of-cause-and-effect.md`. The applied-causal-inference book pairs IV with DML for high-dimensional first stages.

## B. Domain examples and the canonical failure/success cases

### B1. Larkin et al. 2017 — AMC detailing restrictions (the opener's real study)
19 AMCs in 5 states restricted pharma-rep detailing visits (mostly meal/gift bans) between 2006 and 2012; a difference-in-differences/event-study design on physician prescribing found the restrictions **caused a shift from detailed branded drugs toward cheaper generics** — 8 of 11 AMCs that prohibited visits+gifts saw significant reductions in detailed-drug prescribing vs. 1 of 8 that did not restrict. This is the model natural experiment for the chapter: a dated, plausibly exogenous access rule, neighbor/non-restricting controls, a clean outcome. Larkin, Loewenstein et al., *JAMA* 317(17):1785–1795, 2017 (https://www.cmu.edu/dietrich/sds/docs/loewenstein/jama_Larkin_2017.pdf; PMC5815013).

### B2. King et al. 2013 — medical-school gift-restriction policies (published DiD)
A difference-in-differences analysis found medical-school gift-restriction policies were associated with reduced prescribing of newly marketed psychotropic medications by physicians trained under those policies. A clean, citable DiD on promotion policy. (PMC3623604, https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC3623604/.) Complementary state-policy study: gift bans/restrictions associated with lower uptake of new costly psychotropics, larger effect than disclosure alone (Sciencedirect S0277953616306190 / PMID 27856120) — `[verify]` exact effect sizes before quoting.

### B3. Dafny, Ody & Schmitt 2017 — co-pay coupons & generic substitution (cross-state legality as the design)
Copay coupons **increase branded sales by 60%+, entirely by reducing bioequivalent-generic sales**; identification exploits **cross-state and cross-consumer variation in coupon legality** (coupons banned in Medicare; varying state rules). Estimated +$700M–$2.7B total spending for their sample of drugs facing generic entry 2007–2010. Dafny, Ody, Schmitt, "When Discounts Raise Costs: The Effect of Copay Coupons on Generic Utilization," *AEJ: Economic Policy* 9(2):91–123, 2017 (NBER w22745, https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w22745.pdf). This is the template for Ch 13 Track D's coupon causal estimate. (Note: the Ch 1 notes cite a related NBER w29735 update — confirm which vintage/effect size the chapter uses; both exist — `[verify]`.)

### B4. LOE / generic entry as a brand-persistence natural experiment
At loss of exclusivity, small-molecule brands typically erode steeply via generic substitution; brand equity persistence varies by market type (specialty, biologic/biosimilar, high-risk therapy). Generic-entry date is exogenous and dated → a natural experiment for "does brand equity slow erosion after controlling for clinical value?" (Track B). Higher pre-entry sales and shorter exclusivity attract more generic entry (academic generic-entry literature). Source: `pantry/pharma-brand-measurement-synthesis.md` §4 (LOE economics; co-pay-card complication; Georgetown brief on Dafny et al.).

### B5. FAILURE CASE — the naive TWFE staggered-DiD (the methodological trap the chapter must teach)
A Fellow studying staggered state gift bans runs a two-way-fixed-effects regression (unit + year FE, a treatment dummy) and reports a single coefficient. Because effects vary by adoption cohort and over time, TWFE uses *already-treated* states as controls for *later-treated* states ("forbidden comparisons"), and the estimate can be wrong in sign and magnitude (Goodman-Bacon). The fix: a Callaway–Sant'Anna group-time ATT with not-yet-treated controls + an event-study pre-trends plot. This is the single most important "do it right" lesson of the chapter and a perfect five-part AI-validation exercise (ask an LLM for "staggered DiD code," then catch that it hands you TWFE).

## E. Teaching considerations

- **Lead with the design, not the dataset.** The opener (state gift ban + neighbor control) teaches that the *contribution is the identification argument*; the data is commodity. Counter the Fellow's instinct to start from "what's in Part D."
- **Teach the failure first (B5).** Have students run naive TWFE, see a plausible-looking number, then run Callaway–Sant'Anna and the event-study plot and watch it change. The "fails first" pattern matches the book's Ch 8 pedagogy.
- **A decision tree maps question → design:** policy adopted at different dates across units → staggered DiD; a sharp eligibility/spending cutoff → RDD; an exogenous shifter of exposure with a defensible exclusion restriction → IV; a single dated shock with a clean control group → 2×2 DiD / event study. Make students justify the *assumption* each design buys.
- **Named Fellow artifact:** an **identification-design memo** — question, dataset(s), design, the identifying assumption stated explicitly, the diagnostic that would falsify it (pre-trends plot, density test, first-stage F), and the evidence-ladder level it can reach.
- **Five-part AI block hook:** LLM exercise = "draft a staggered-DiD analysis plan + code"; AI-validation = catch that it returned biased TWFE and demand a heterogeneity-robust estimator and a pre-trends check (the load-bearing human judgment).

## G. Gaps and flags

- **G1. Open Payments is association-grade alone.** Payment↔prescribing correlation is real and replicated but confounded (physicians who take meals may differ on patient mix, specialty, volume). Any causal claim needs a design (A2–A5). Flag in every brief that uses it.
- **G2. Part D does not directly observe NBRx / new starts.** NBRx (new-to-brand) is a longitudinal-patient construct (IQVIA-style); Part D PUF gives annual claim counts per provider×drug, not patient-level switching. Fellows must *proxy* new starts (e.g., first-appearance heuristics) and label them as proxies — a real limitation for any brand-persistence project. (`pantry/pharma-brand-measurement-synthesis.md` §2.)
- **G3. Medicare/Medicaid population skew.** Part D = older beneficiaries; SDUD = Medicaid case-mix. External validity to the commercial-insured population (where coupons operate) is limited — note when a finding can/can't generalize.
- **G4. ICER extraction + contestation.** ICER reports are PDFs (manual/LLM extraction, validate against the fluency trap, Ch 10) and the QALY method is politically contested (disability/equity critiques; industry pushback). Use ICER as *a* value benchmark, flagged, not as ground truth.
- **G5. IV exclusion restrictions are untestable.** Any IV proposal needs an unusually strong substantive defense; default to skepticism. Weak instruments bias toward OLS — report first-stage F.
- **G6. `[verify]` list:** current ICER $/QALY thresholds; latest Part D PUF year; Dafny et al. citation vintage (w22745 vs w29735) and exact effect sizes; the psychotropic state-policy effect-size range (39–83% market-share reduction cited in press, confirm against PMID 27856120); Gleevec RDD peer-review status (arXiv).
- **G7. Compliance/welfare angle for the chapter:** these designs are also the tools that expose welfare-relevant findings (coupon generic suppression, susceptibility proxies). The chapter is methodologically neutral but feeds the adversarial Track D work — keep the partner-framing tension (TIKTOC Risk 2/4) in view.
