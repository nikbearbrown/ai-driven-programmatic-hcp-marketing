## Preface

Somewhere in a brand team's conference room, right now, a slide is on the screen claiming a "44% script lift." The deck is well-designed. The number is large. Nobody in the room can say what it would take to know whether the number is real — because the platform that served the ads also measured the lift, targeted the physicians most likely to prescribe anyway, and reported the campaigns that worked while the ones that did nothing quietly became "learnings." Every sentence on the slide is true. None of it is evidence. This book exists because that gap — between a true sentence and a causal claim — is now the central problem in pharmaceutical marketing, and almost nobody in the field is trained to see it.

The reason the gap matters is structural, and it is the same anomaly underneath every chapter here. In ordinary advertising the person who decides to buy also pays, so their wallet disciplines the choice. Pharma severs that link: the physician decides, the patient consumes, the payer pays, and the institution constrains the menu. The party who pays is not at the table. Every next-best-action engine in the industry is, at bottom, answering a Rung-1 engagement question — *which physician, which message, which moment* — with impressive machinery. But the question a budget actually asks is causal: *did this spend change a prescription that would not otherwise have changed, and was that change good for the patient?* That is a different and far harder question, and the field has built an entire optimization loop that never has to answer it.

Why now? Because two things arrived at once. The agentic-AI wave gave every vendor a reason to relabel a gradient-boosted tree ensemble as an "AI-powered operating system" or a "mixture of experts" — and gave every brand team a reason to believe the relabeling. At the same time the point-of-care channel — advertising delivered inside the electronic health record, through the same clinical-alert plumbing that carries drug-interaction warnings — crossed a billion dollars in annual spend, with a body of independent, peer-reviewed evidence for its clinical effect that rounds, honestly, to nothing. A field full of confident, attribution-circular vendor claims met a technology that makes confident claims cheaper to produce. The result is a market that urgently needs people who can tell mechanism from effect, association from cause, and a tuned baseline from a routed model that supposedly beats it.

Why us? Because this is the kind of question Humanitarians AI's Fellows are built to take apart, and because the method has a name and a shape. Through the Fellows Program, Humanitarians AI runs a standing pipeline of data-capable people — in data science, design, engineering, the sciences, and business — who learn AI by doing AI: specifying a question, building a proof of concept on public or synthetic data, and being honest about what the test could and could not establish. Nik Bear Brown — who teaches AI, data science, and statistics at Northeastern, holds a PhD in computational biology with the statistical training to match, and founded Humanitarians AI — built the *Irreducibly Human* curriculum around exactly this discipline. This book is one of its case studies: a field where polish is the medium and computational skepticism is the irreducible human work.

> **The innovation-lab offer.**
>
> Bear Brown, LLC and Humanitarians AI together operate as an **external innovation lab** for companies. The model is simple and deliberately low-commitment. We research and prototype the AI that *might* help you: we scope the question, build a proof of concept on public or synthetic data, and test whether it actually holds — *before* you build a team around it. If something proves promising, we work alongside your own people to implement it. Because Humanitarians AI runs 100–200 Fellows at any time across many majors, when a project needs extra hands for a few weeks it can be staffed on a per-hour basis. The contract that makes this safe is an **IP firewall**: our published work runs on public and synthetic data only, and your team replicates anything promising on your proprietary data, internally, on your side of the wall. You get high-evidence decisions at low commitment, and your IP never leaves the building.
>
> **This book is that lab's method written down.** Chapter 11 is literally the external-innovation-lab operating model; Chapters 12 and 13 are how a Fellow runs a public-data research portfolio under the firewall; Chapter 14 is the one-page brief that turns a prototype into a go/kill decision. If you want to see what working with the lab looks like before you ever talk to us, read those four chapters and run the exercises. That *is* the engagement.

A word on what this book does **not** cover. It is not a HIPAA or regulatory-compliance manual — it flags where MLR review, fair balance, the Sunshine Act, and HIPAA are triggered, but it routes the adjudication to counsel rather than rendering legal judgments. It is not a deep-learning textbook; the architecture chapters are about *what works on tabular HCP data and what is merely marketing*, not about training large models from scratch. It does not teach media buying, creative production, or the operational mechanics of running a campaign. And it takes no position on whether any individual drug should be promoted — it teaches you to ask whether the *evidence* for a promotional claim is causal, and whether the prescribing change it produces is good for the patient. Those last two questions are the whole book. Everything else is in service of them.
