## Acknowledgments

This book was pressure-tested by the people who had to live inside its discipline. The Humanitarians AI Fellows ran its methods on public data, found the places where a clean argument met a messy dataset, and were honest when the data would not support the strong version of a claim — which is the hardest and most valuable thing a researcher can do. ## Glossary

A working reference for the principal terms this book uses. One line each; see the named chapter for full development.

**NBA (Next-Best-Action)** — the engine that recommends which physician to engage, with which message, through which channel, next; it answers a Rung-1 engagement question, not the causal budget question. *(Chapters 1, 4.)*

**Split agency** — the structural anomaly of pharma: the physician decides, the patient consumes, the payer pays, and the institution constrains the menu, so the party who pays is absent from the decision. *(Chapter 1.)*

**NPI (National Provider Identifier)** — the permanent, deterministic 10-digit federal identifier for a US provider; the spine the targeting stack hangs on, categorically unlike a probabilistic web cookie. *(Chapters 1, 3.)*

**HCP identity graph** — the persistent, cross-channel profile maintained against each NPI: which messages a physician saw, through which channels, and what prescribing followed. *(Chapter 3.)*

**NRx / TRx / NBRx** — new prescriptions, total prescriptions, and new-to-brand prescriptions; the prescribing measures campaigns are graded against. *(Chapter 2.)*

**Open Payments** — the CMS database of industry transfers of value to physicians, reported under the Sunshine Act; both a compliance mechanism and a primary public research dataset. *(Chapters 1, 11, 12.)*

**CDS Hooks / SMART on FHIR** — the clinical-interoperability standards, built for safety alerts, that commercial platforms use to deliver branded messages inside the EHR. *(Chapter 1.)*

**Co-pay card** — a manufacturer coupon that lowers the patient's out-of-pocket cost; improves individual affordability while suppressing generic substitution and raising total system cost. *(Chapter 1.)*

**Lift vs. brand** — the two dependent variables a campaign moves: short-term incremental prescribing (lift) versus durable physician association (brand); they require different evidence designs. *(Chapter 2.)*

**Lift vs. incrementality** — observed lift is a difference between groups; incrementality is the part of that difference the intervention actually caused, recoverable only with a control. *(Chapters 5, 8.)*

**Uplift / Qini** — uplift modeling estimates the treatment effect per physician (the persuadables); the Qini curve measures how well it ranks them. *(Chapter 8.)*

**Attribution circularity** — the structural conflict in which the platform that serves an ad also measures its lift, with no firewall between the interest in a high number and its production. *(Chapters 1, 5.)*

**Selection on the outcome** — targeting the physicians already most likely to prescribe, then crediting their prescribing to the ad; the first and largest source of inflated lift. *(Chapter 5.)*

**Evidence taxonomy (two axes)** — grading a claim on study-design strength and source independence, taking the minimum of the two as the grade. *(Chapter 5.)*

**Evidence Ladder** — this book's own construct (Levels 0–6) for where an artifact stands; public-data work tops out near Level 3, and the rungs above require the partner's proprietary data. *(Chapters 11, 13.)*

**MoE (Mixture of Experts)** — a routing architecture with specialized sub-models and a gate; in HCP marketing the term is often a relabeled stacked ensemble, legitimate ML but not MoE. *(Chapter 7.)*

**Calibration** — whether a model's predicted probabilities match observed frequencies (Brier score, reliability diagram); often more decisive than AUC for targeting. *(Chapters 6, 13.)*

**Difference-in-differences (DiD)** — a quasi-experimental design comparing treated and control trends around a shock; modern heterogeneity-robust estimators (Callaway–Sant'Anna) replace naive two-way fixed effects with clean controls. *(Chapters 8, 12, 13.)*

**IP firewall** — the contract that makes lab work safe: published research uses public/synthetic data only, the partner replicates promising methods on proprietary data internally, and nothing confidential crosses into the output. *(Chapter 11.)*

**Pre-registered kill criterion** — the result that would make you abandon a project, written down *before* you see the result, so a disappointing finding cannot be relabeled a success. *(Chapters 11, 13.)*

**MLR (Medical-Legal-Regulatory) review** — the mandatory cross-functional review of promotional content; the lab flags the trigger and routes adjudication to counsel. *(Chapter 11.)*

**One Drug, End to End** — the book's running project: a single branded drug carried through every chapter, from split-agency map to partner handoff brief. *(All chapters.)*
