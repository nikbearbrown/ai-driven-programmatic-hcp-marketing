# Chapter 2 — Lift vs. Brand: The Two Dependent Variables

## Chapter overview

By the end of this chapter you will be able to look at any commercial goal in pharma HCP marketing and decide which of two fundamentally different questions it is really asking: is it a question about campaign **lift** — the incremental prescriptions an exposure caused — or about **brand formation** — whether a drug becomes a physician's durable default for a particular kind of patient? These are not two readings of one number; they are different constructs that demand different metrics, different study designs, and different time horizons. You will be able to name the standard prescribing metrics (TRx, NRx, NBRx, persistence, loyalty deciles, share of voice, share of mind), pick the right one for a stated goal, and spot when a tempting metric — engagement above all — is a misleading surrogate. This is the chapter the rest of the book routes through, and it is where you choose your own research thread.

## Learning objectives

By the end of this chapter you should be able to:

1. **(Understand)** Define TRx, NRx, NBRx / new starts, persistence, loyalty deciles, share of voice, and share of mind.
2. **(Evaluate)** Judge when a metric — especially engagement — is a misleading surrogate for the outcome that actually matters.
3. **(Apply / Part B)** Map a stated commercial goal to a primary outcome metric and its failure modes — and choose your own research thread (a lift thread or a brand thread) for the rest of the book.

## Opening case — winning refills, losing decisions

A brand team is in a good mood. Their drug — a mature cardiovascular brand, several years on the market — is posting healthy total prescription numbers. The monthly dashboard is green. Refills are strong, the install base is loyal, the top line looks stable. By the metric on the wall, the brand is winning.

Then an analyst pulls a different cut. Among prescriptions written for patients *new to the brand* — the people deciding, right now, what to start — the brand is down 20% year over year. New patients are going to competitors. The strong top line is the sound of an existing base refilling old decisions; the *new* decisions are quietly being lost.

This is the failure the chapter turns on. The team was watching the wrong dependent variable. Total volume was a lagging measure of decisions already made; it can stay green for a year or more on refill inertia while the brand's actual competitive position erodes underneath it. The phrase to remember: **winning refills, losing decisions.** Whether you would have caught this depends entirely on whether you knew there were two questions hiding inside "is the brand doing well?" — and which metric answers which.

## Core concept 1 — The two dependent variables: lift vs. brand

Everything in this book routes through one split, which is why this is one of three load-bearing chapters.

**Lift** is the *incremental* change in prescriptions caused by a specific exposure. It is a short-horizon, campaign-attribution question: *did this ad cause more scripts than would have happened anyway?* The key word is *incremental* — not "did scripts go up," but "did scripts go up *because of this*, beyond the counterfactual where the exposure never happened."

**Brand** is a *durable belief-and-behavior system*. It asks whether the drug becomes the physician's default mental and clinical choice for a particular patient archetype, and whether that choice persists, survives competition and loss of exclusivity, and sustains a price premium. Campaign attribution asks "did this exposure generate incremental scripts?" Brand measurement asks "is this drug becoming the default choice for a category of physicians and patients?"

The misconception worth killing early: "lift and brand are the same thing measured at different time scales." They are not. They are different *constructs* requiring different study designs. Lift needs a **control or holdout** — a comparison group that was *not* exposed — because incrementality is inherently causal (Chapter 8). Brand needs measurement of **durable association over time** — a longitudinal design (Chapter 9). And crucially, the two can come apart in both directions: a campaign can produce lift with zero brand formation (a one-time trial that never repeats), and brand can grow with no measurable campaign lift at all (a guideline change, or diffusion through key opinion leaders).

Take a single content variant and notice it generates two entirely different studies. (a) The lift question: "did exposed NPIs write more new starts than matched *unexposed* NPIs over the next 90 days?" (b) The brand question: "did exposed physicians durably shift toward associating Brand X with *this* patient type, measured 6–12 months out?" Same input, two dependent variables, two designs (framing from `pharma-brand-measurement-synthesis.md` and the book's arc statement).

## Core concept 2 — The prescribing-volume metrics: TRx, NRx, NBRx

You cannot reason about either dependent variable without the vocabulary of prescription counting. The three core metrics look similar and mean very different things.

- **TRx (Total Rx):** every prescription dispensed — new prescriptions *plus* refills. It is the headline number, and it is **lagging**. TRx can look healthy on refill inertia while the brand is aging, because a prescription written today for a patient who started two years ago counts the same as a fresh decision.
- **NRx (New Rx):** newly *written* prescriptions, excluding refills. Better — but still imperfect, because it can include a patient already on the brand who simply gets a new script written.
- **NBRx / NBx (New-to-Brand Rx):** prescriptions where the patient is genuinely **new to the brand**, established by looking *back* across the patient's prior therapies over a longitudinal window. This is the cleanest read on *current* treatment decisions. NBRx has subtypes worth knowing: new-to-market / therapy-naïve, switch-from-competitor, restart-after-lapse, and add-on.

The misconception is the opening case in miniature: "rising TRx means the brand is winning." TRx can rise on refill persistence — old decisions — while NBRx falls. **NBRx is the leading indicator** because it reflects the physician's decision *at the moment of choice*, and it reflects the marketing team's effort most directly. The brand team in the opening case had flat-or-rising TRx and NBRx down 20%: the install base was refilling while every new patient went to a competitor. Equity was eroding even though the dashboard was green — which, paradoxically, is exactly the moment a brand team should worry most.

(The metric definitions are standardized across the major data vendors. IQVIA's Brand Analytics breaks NBRx into the subtypes above and positions its NBRx as a "gold-standard" longitudinal input with roughly 93% outpatient prescription coverage before projection — but note that "gold-standard" and "93% coverage" are **IQVIA's own characterizations of its product**, accurate as descriptions of positioning, not as independent validation. See also Definitive Healthcare's NBRx glossary and Alpha Sophia's NBRx definition, which agree that NBRx reflects marketing effort and competitive share capture more directly than NRx or TRx.) ## Core concept 3 — Persistence, new starts, and prescriber loyalty deciles

Volume is not the whole story; *who* prescribes and whether their patients *stay* matter just as much.

**New patient starts** are the flow of newly-treated patients (closely related to NBRx). **Persistence** (also adherence, or DOT, days of therapy) asks whether started patients stay on the drug — a campaign that wins trials but loses persistence has built nothing durable. **Loyalty deciles** segment the universe of eligible prescribers in a therapeutic class by prescribing volume or value: decile 10 is the highest-volume prescribers, decile 1 the lowest, and non-writers fall outside. Sophisticated teams go a step further than raw volume and compute **brand share within each physician's eligible patient pool** — Brand X scripts divided by all eligible category scripts — which yields behavioral segments: non-writer, trialist, occasional writer, loyalist, advocate/KOL.

Here is the misconception that costs real money: "always target the highest-volume (decile 10) prescribers." Not necessarily. A decile-10 physician with *low* brand share may be a *better* target than a decile-5 physician with high share — because the upside, the conversion headroom, is bigger. Consider two cardiologists. Dr. X is decile 10 with 5% brand share; Dr. Y is decile 5 with 60% brand share. Dr. X is the larger opportunity: there is far more of their prescribing left to convert. A naïve propensity model that simply ranks physicians by *predicted prescribing* would rank Dr. Y higher and miss Dr. X's headroom entirely.

That last sentence is a seed. It is the first appearance of the lesson Chapter 8 will make central: a model that predicts *who will prescribe* is not a model that predicts *whom you can change*, and targeting the people already most likely to prescribe — then measuring their response — systematically *inflates* your lift estimate. The goal of brand work is converting *habit*, not generating one isolated script (segmentation framing from `pharma-brand-measurement-synthesis.md` §2B; predictive-KPI framing from PM360, "Every Metric Tells a Story").

## Core concept 4 — Share of Voice vs. Share of Mind

Now lift the lens from individual prescribers to the market.

**Share of Voice (SOV)**, or detail share, is the brand's share of *promotional presence* in a market — its slice of all rep calls, details, speaker programs, emails, digital impressions, journal ads, and congress presence relative to competitors. It measures how loud you are.

**Share of Mind (SOM)** is the brand's share of *physician mental availability* — recall, association, prescribing intent. It measures whether the noise landed. The industry shorthand **SOMi → SOMa** captures a claimed dynamic: share-of-mind-*intent* (what physicians say they think and intend) is claimed to *lead* share-of-mind-*actual* / market share, with a lag.

There is a famous rule of thumb borrowed from fast-moving consumer goods (FMCG): if SOV exceeds SOM, the brand tends to grow; if SOV is below SOM, it tends to shrink. The misconception is to treat this as a *law* in pharma. It is, at most, a **leading indicator, not a law**, because pharma inserts gates that FMCG does not have: the FDA label, clinical guidelines, payer access, prior authorization, safety signals, and institutional pathways. Mental-availability ideas from the Ehrenberg-Bass tradition apply to salience and reach, but far less to *evidence-gated* prescribing.

Picture a brand with 30% SOV but only 15% SOM. FMCG logic predicts growth — you are shouting twice as loud as you are remembered, so memory should catch up. But if that brand sits on Tier-3 formulary behind step therapy, the growth never materializes. The gate, not the voice, is binding. As the teaching analogy goes: shouting louder helps only if the door isn't locked by the formulary.

The SOMi → SOMa leading-indicator claim is also the chapter's flagged research finding. It is **asserted qualitatively across the industry but not rigorously tested on linked survey-plus-claims data.** The corroboration that exists is thin and vendor-sourced — for example, Inizio's statement that "higher brand equity is predictive of market-share growth" is a vendor claim with no published test attached [verify; treat as a hypothesis, not a finding]. Whether mind-share actually predicts share growth with a lag is a tractable study a Fellow could run on public data (Chapter 13).

## Core concept 5 — "Brand = remembering the patient type"

Here is the chapter's deepest and least intuitive idea, and one this book takes a position on [the brand-equity-vs-clinical-value construct is flagged, not asserted].

Pharma brand equity is **not awareness.** The FMCG version of brand is identity expression — "I wear Calvin Klein." The pharma version is something far more constrained and far more valuable: *"For this patient profile, in this disease state, after this prior therapy, Brand X is my default."* Equity is the durable pattern where a physician mentally associates a drug with a specific *patient type / clinical situation / risk profile / treatment-sequence position* — and then repeatedly prescribes it for that type. Equity is strongest not when the physician remembers the *brand*, but when the physician remembers the *patient type* and the brand comes along automatically.

The misconception: "brand equity equals aided or unaided awareness." Awareness is necessary but nowhere near sufficient. The strongest equity signal is patient-archetype ownership, and it shows up *behaviorally* — as high share of a physician's eligible patients — not in a recall survey. Consider a physician who says, sincerely, "Brand X has the best efficacy data," but who has never once switched a stable patient through three successive generic entries. Strong *stated* awareness; weak *behavioral* equity. (This is exactly Chapter 9's opening case, where we escalate from defining the construct to designing the measurement.)

Keep one distinction sharp throughout. The evidence that marketing builds durable brand *identity* — as opposed to mere *association with prescribing* — is weaker than the association evidence. The synthesis is explicit: the evidence is "stronger for association and behavioral influence than for clean causal proof of brand identity" (`pharma-brand-measurement-synthesis.md`). Do not let "brand equity" smuggle in a causal claim the data does not support.

## Worked example — mapping a goal to a metric (and finding its failure mode)

**Situation.** A brand director says: "Our goal next year is to grow share." You are asked to turn that into a measurable primary outcome with a defined failure mode, so the team is not fooled the way the opening-case team was.

**Analytical process (including the dead ends).**

First instinct: use TRx — it's the headline number everyone watches. Dead end. We just saw TRx stay green for a year while the brand was losing every new decision. TRx as the primary outcome would have *hidden* the problem the director is implicitly worried about.

Second instinct: use NRx, since "growth" sounds like "new prescriptions." Better, but NRx still counts existing-brand patients getting fresh scripts, so it blends old decisions with new ones. It would understate the erosion.

Third step: choose **NBRx** as the primary outcome — new-to-brand prescriptions, the cleanest read on the decisions being made right now. But a metric without a named failure mode is a trap. NBRx's failure mode is **trial without persistence**: you can win a burst of new starts that never refill, posting strong NBRx while building no durable base. So you pair the primary metric with a guardrail: NBRx *and* persistence / DOT over the following two to three fills.

**Resolution.** "Grow share" becomes: *increase NBRx year over year, with persistence held at or above current levels.* You have converted a vague goal into a measurable outcome with an explicit failure mode the metric alone would miss.

**The lesson (one sentence).** Every commercial goal hides a specific dependent variable and a specific way of being fooled — naming both is the entire skill.

**The limit (where it fails).** This mapping tells you *what to measure*; it does not tell you whether your campaign *caused* the NBRx you observe. NBRx going up is consistent with a great campaign, a competitor's stumble, a guideline change, or seasonal noise. Establishing causation is the lift question, and it needs a control group you do not get from a dashboard — which is precisely why Chapter 8 exists.

## Common misconceptions

**"Lift and brand are the same thing on different time scales."** Plausible, because brand sounds like "lift that lasts." It fails because they are different constructs with different designs — lift needs a control group (causal), brand needs longitudinal association measurement — and they routinely come apart. Tie back to the opening case: a campaign could have produced short-term lift there while brand equity (new decisions) collapsed.

**"Rising TRx means the brand is winning."** Plausible, because TRx is the biggest, most visible number. It fails because TRx is lagging and refill-loaded; it can stay green on old decisions while NBRx — the new decisions — falls. This *is* the opening case.

**"Always target the highest-decile prescribers."** Plausible, because they write the most. It fails because high volume with high existing brand share means low conversion headroom; a high-volume / low-share physician is often the bigger opportunity. And ranking by predicted prescribing seeds the inflated-lift error of Chapter 8.

## Evidence check

- **Independent (peer-reviewed / governmental):** the *association* between industry payments and increased branded prescribing (Annals of Internal Medicine systematic review; a JAMA Internal Medicine statin study) — robust as association, but **not** clean causal proof of *brand identity* formation. The metric definitions (TRx/NRx/NBRx/deciles/SOV) are industry-standardized and uncontroversial as definitions.
- **Vendor-generated:** IQVIA's "gold-standard NBRx" and "~93% coverage" (product self-description); Inizio's "brand equity predicts market-share growth" (an assertion with no published test); any specific SOMi → SOMa predictive claim. Cite these as vendor positioning, never as independent validation.
- **Hypothetical:** the opening-case brand and the worked-example dialogue are illustrative composites built from documented dynamics.
- **Contested:** **brand equity vs. clinical value** is an open empirical question [contested — see pantry] — it may be uncorrelated or even negatively correlated. A BMJ-linked analysis argued the most-*promoted* drugs were *less* likely than top-selling drugs to be effective, safe, affordable, novel, or genuine advances; a 2023 study reported that 68% of the most-advertised drugs have low added clinical benefit. [verify both the BMJ-linked analysis and the 2023 "68%" figure against their primary sources] Do not assert a correlation in either direction; it is testable against ICER value scores (Chapters 9 and 13).
- **`[verify]` flags:** the IQVIA coverage figure; the Inizio claim; the BMJ-linked and 2023 promotion-vs-value figures.

The flagged research finding for this chapter: **SOMi → SOMa predictive validity is not rigorously validated on linked survey-plus-claims data.** Present it as a hypothesis to be tested (Chapter 13), not a settled fact.

## Compliance & patient-welfare check

**MLR / FDA fair balance.** Brand-measurement work is mostly internal analytics and does not itself trigger MLR review — but the *content variants* whose effects you measure absolutely do. Any creative tested for lift or association must carry fair balance (benefit and risk at comparable prominence); a test design that optimizes purely for NBRx can quietly select the creative that under-weights risk. Flag the creative, route to MLR.

**Privacy.** Metric work runs on de-identified-patient, NPI-keyed claims data (the legal substrate is Chapter 3's subject). The privacy exposure here is low at the *measurement* layer, but note that linking survey share-of-mind data back to individual physicians' claims (the SOMi → SOMa study) raises identity-linkage questions worth flagging to counsel before the data is joined.

**Patient-welfare note.** The welfare risk in this chapter is *metric capture*: optimizing NBRx and share without ever measuring whether the new starts were *clinically appropriate*. The synthesis is blunt that AI sees commercial KPIs well (NBRx, switch-ins, SOV) and patient-welfare KPIs poorly (real-world outcomes, clinical appropriateness, total cost of care). The welfare question for every Fellow project: *does this metric, optimized hard, reward anything a patient would not want?* The opening-case fix — winning new decisions — is good for the brand; whether it is good for patients depends on whether those new decisions were the right drug, which the commercial metric never asks.

## Five-part AI exercise block

**When to use AI.** Use an LLM to draft definitions, generate candidate failure modes for a metric, or restate a vague commercial goal as several alternative measurable outcomes you can then choose among. Good for breadth and first drafts.

**When NOT to use AI.** Do not let an LLM decide *whether your thread is a lift or a brand question*, or *whether brand equity tracks clinical value* — the first requires understanding your specific intervention's causal structure, the second is a contested empirical question the model will happily answer with false confidence.

**LLM exercise (copy-paste prompt):**
> "I have a pharma commercial goal: [STATE GOAL]. Propose three different primary outcome metrics it could map to (choose from TRx, NRx, NBRx, persistence/DOT, loyalty-decile movement, share of voice, share of mind). For each, state (a) what it measures, (b) its single most important failure mode, and (c) a guardrail metric that would catch that failure. Then tell me which metrics make this a *lift* question and which make it a *brand* question. Do not assert that any metric reflects patient benefit."

**CLI exercise.** From a public prescriber dataset (e.g., a Medicare Part D Prescriber file you will use heavily in Chapter 12), use the command line to compute, for a single drug, the count of prescribers and total claims by year, then the year-over-year change. Write one sentence on why this Part-D-derived number is closer to a TRx-style lagging measure than an NBRx leading measure, and what you would need to approximate NBRx.

**AI Validation exercise.** Ask an LLM to summarize the relationship between share of voice and share of mind in pharma. Then check its answer against this chapter: did it state the SOV > SOM rule as a *law* (wrong for pharma) or as a *leading indicator gated by formulary/label/guidelines* (right)? ## What would change my mind

The central claim of this chapter is that lift and brand are genuinely distinct constructs that require distinct measurement, and that NBRx — not TRx — is the leading indicator of brand health. A study showing that, in a large linked dataset, TRx and NBRx move together so tightly that the distinction makes no practical difference to brand decisions would substantially weaken the "winning refills, losing decisions" framing and the insistence on NBRx. Separately, if a rigorous test on linked survey-plus-claims data showed that share-of-mind-intent does *not* predict subsequent share growth, the entire SOMi → SOMa leading-indicator concept — and the brand-measurement practices built on it — would need to be demoted from "leading indicator" to "interesting but non-predictive attitude data."

## Still puzzling

- Does brand equity, measured properly, track clinical value (ICER scores) at all — and if it is uncorrelated or negatively correlated, what does that say about what the commercial machine is actually optimizing? (Seeds Chapters 9 and 13.)
- The evidence for marketing building durable brand *identity* is weaker than for association with prescribing. Is durable identity even separable from "repeated exposure plus inertia," or is "brand equity" partly a story we tell about persistence we cannot otherwise explain?
- At loss of exclusivity, small-molecule brand equity usually collapses but specialty/biologic equity often persists — is the persistent part really *brand belief*, or is it service infrastructure (patient support, hubs, biosimilar friction) wearing brand's clothing? (Seeds the Chapter 13 LOE natural-experiment thread.)

## Chapter summary

You can now tell, for any commercial goal, whether it is a lift question or a brand question — and you know why that distinction is not pedantic but determines your entire study design. You can define and correctly deploy TRx, NRx, NBRx, persistence, loyalty deciles, share of voice, and share of mind, and you know why NBRx, not TRx, reads the decisions being made now. You can spot the high-decile-targeting trap and connect it forward to the inflated-lift problem. You understand that pharma brand equity is "remembering the patient type," not awareness, and you hold the contested brand-vs-clinical-value question open rather than asserting it. And you have chosen your own research thread — lift or brand — which you will carry to the final handoff brief.

## Key terms

- **Lift:** the incremental change in prescriptions *caused* by a specific exposure; a short-horizon causal question needing a control group.
- **Brand (equity):** a durable belief-and-behavior system in which a drug becomes the physician's default for a patient archetype; a longitudinal construct.
- **TRx / NRx / NBRx:** total prescriptions (incl. refills) / new written prescriptions / new-to-brand prescriptions established by longitudinal lookback; NBRx is the leading decision indicator.
- **Persistence (DOT):** whether started patients stay on therapy; the guardrail against winning trials but losing durable use.
- **Loyalty decile:** segmentation of eligible prescribers by volume (decile 10 highest); best paired with brand share to find conversion headroom.
- **Share of Voice (SOV):** the brand's share of total promotional presence in a market.
- **Share of Mind (SOM); SOMi → SOMa:** the brand's share of physician mental availability; the claimed (and unvalidated) dynamic that intent leads actual share with a lag.
- **Patient-archetype ownership:** the strongest equity signal — the physician remembering the patient type, not just the brand name.
